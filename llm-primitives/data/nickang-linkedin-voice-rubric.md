## Core voice summary

Nick's recent writing voice is:

- personal first, abstract second
- reflective but not vague
- direct, opinionated, and willing to sound human rather than polished
- concrete and example-driven, often starting from a lived scene or recent event
- conceptually tidy: he likes naming the lesson, tension, analogy, or mechanism
- crisp and punchy at the sentence level, with frequent rhetorical questions and emphatic turns
- warm but not soft; sharp, sometimes combative, but usually in service of clarity
- practical even when reflective: many posts try to extract a takeaway, rule, or framing
- comfortable thinking out loud in public before certainty is fully formed
- increasingly shaped by solo-founder experimentation, AI tooling, audience growth, money, and freedom

For LinkedIn specifically, the best adaptation is not "corporate Nick." It is
Nick's blog voice compressed into a more public-facing professional format
without sanding off the candor.

## What the evaluator should reward

Score each dimension from `1` to `5`, where `5` means strongly aligned with the
voice and `1` means clearly unlike it.

### 1. Lived-entry opening

Does the post begin from something observed, experienced, tested, or recently
 noticed, instead of opening with generic thought-leadership abstraction?

High-scoring traits:

- opens with "I", "recently", "today", "I noticed", "I realised", or a concrete scene
- may open with a high-energy conversational line if it quickly grounds itself
- starts from an anecdote, work incident, parenting moment, product observation, or small tension
- feels like the writer earned the insight through contact with life

Low-scoring traits:

- opens with "In today's fast-paced world..."
- opens with a broad thesis that could have been written by anyone
- sounds like a repackaged lesson with no originating moment

### 2. Concrete-to-general movement

Does the piece move from specific example to broader insight in a clean,
 believable way?

High-scoring traits:

- uses one concrete example and extracts a larger lesson from it
- names the tension explicitly
- can include a "here's where I am now" progress update before zooming out
- turns observation into a portable idea without over-explaining

Low-scoring traits:

- stays trapped in anecdote with no takeaway
- jumps straight to high-level advice without grounding
- feels like disconnected mini-points rather than one coherent arc

### 3. Clarity and sentence rhythm

Does it sound clear, legible, and spoken by a thoughtful human rather than a
 content machine?

High-scoring traits:

- mostly short-to-medium sentences
- occasional punchy single-line paragraph for emphasis
- simple words over inflated vocabulary
- tidy transitions such as "Here's the thing", "The point is", "What I learned", "This is where..."
- tolerates a little spoken-energy messiness if the writing still feels alive

Low-scoring traits:

- too many stacked clauses
- MBA or consultancy phrasing
- heavy use of abstract nouns without images or examples

### 4. Voice confidence and opinionation

Does the writer sound like he is actually taking a position?

High-scoring traits:

- willing to say "this is nonsense", "this is the problem", "I think", "I suspect", "I've come to realise"
- makes judgments, but usually from reasoning or lived evidence
- can sound sharp, amused, or lightly provocative
- does not hide uncertainty when uncertainty is the honest state

Low-scoring traits:

- hedges every point into nothing
- over-balances every sentence with caveats
- sounds afraid of having a real opinion

### 5. Human texture

Does the post retain ordinary human texture instead of becoming pure polished
 insight?

High-scoring traits:

- includes small imperfections, admissions, doubts, or self-corrections
- shows curiosity, uncertainty, or change of mind
- may include a rhetorical aside, parenthetical clarification, or informal phrase
- sometimes lets the reader see the writer mid-deliberation, not only after the lesson is polished

Low-scoring traits:

- too sterile
- too polished and "finished"
- reads like a brand team approved every sentence

### 6. Conceptual packaging

Does the post package the idea into a memorable framing, analogy, or named
 tension?

High-scoring traits:

- gives the reader a handle: "raising floor", "capsicum analogy", "brain surge", "wheelhouse"
- can use a striking contrast or memorable phrase instead of a formal analogy
- states the lesson in a compact way
- makes the idea easy to recall later

Low-scoring traits:

- interesting observations but no clear framing
- generic "3 lessons" packaging with no distinctive concept

### 7. Anti-corporate tone

Does the post avoid generic LinkedIn polish and performative professionalism?

High-scoring traits:

- sounds like a person, not a content strategy deck
- avoids overused business phrases
- can be earnest without sounding sanctimonious
- can be ambitious, energetic, or even a bit boastful without sounding fake

Low-scoring traits:

- "unlock", "leverage", "journey", "game-changer", "incredible founders", "grateful to announce"
- performative humility or motivational fluff
- obvious attempt to optimize for applause

### 8. Practicality of takeaway

Even when reflective, does the post leave the reader with a usable takeaway,
 decision rule, or changed framing?

High-scoring traits:

- lands on a lesson, heuristic, warning, or perspective shift
- gives the sense that the writer writes to think, not just to signal
- even founder-update posts should leave behind an insight, not just a diary entry

Low-scoring traits:

- all mood, no point
- generic inspiration with no learning value

## Strong recurring patterns in the writing

These should be treated as positive evidence, not mandatory checkboxes.

- Starts from a recent event, tiny story, or observed problem.
- Often starts with momentum or urgency, then earns the abstraction.
- Talks in first person and often lets the reader watch the thought process.
- Uses rhetorical questions to create movement.
- Names tensions explicitly: freedom vs income, speed vs certainty, durability vs reach, originality vs copying what works, abstraction vs fundamentals.
- Builds small analogies from everyday objects or situations.
- Uses occasional strong phrases and mild irreverence when conviction is high.
- Alternates between intimate reflection and practical explanation.
- Shares numbers, timelines, concrete metrics, and progress markers when relevant.
- Is willing to say "I don't know," "I nearly gave up," or "this is me thinking out loud."
- Often closes by extracting a portable principle.

## Things to avoid when generating in this voice

- Generic inspirational LinkedIn voice.
- "I am humbled/excited/grateful to share..." openings.
- Hashtags, emoji, or engagement bait.
- Listicles unless the source idea naturally demands one.
- Overly smooth motivational cadence.
- Empty certainty that skips the messy human part.
- Excessive polish that removes edge, doubt, or specificity.
- Sounding like a founder-brand ghostwriter.
- Fake vulnerability that arrives pre-packaged as a lesson.
- Sanitising the money, ambition, status, or ego dimensions when they are clearly part of the real post.

## LinkedIn adaptation guidance

The target is not a blog post pasted into LinkedIn. The target is:

- 3 to 6 short paragraphs
- one idea only
- one concrete anchor
- one clean takeaway
- no hashtags
- no bullet lists unless absolutely necessary
- readable in under a minute
- allowed to sound like an honest field note from someone building in public

The writing should still feel like Nick, but slightly tighter and more legible
 for a scrolling audience.

## Suggested evaluator scoring rubric

Use this weighting when scoring a generated LinkedIn draft:

- `Lived-entry opening`: 15
- `Concrete-to-general movement`: 20
- `Clarity and sentence rhythm`: 15
- `Voice confidence and opinionation`: 15
- `Human texture`: 10
- `Conceptual packaging`: 10
- `Anti-corporate tone`: 10
- `Practicality of takeaway`: 5

Total score: `100`

## Suggested evaluator verdict bands

- `90-100`: Strong Nick-like voice. Publishable after light human editing.
- `75-89`: Mostly aligned. Good draft, but needs sharper wording or more lived specificity.
- `60-74`: Partial match. Core idea may be usable, but the voice is too generic, too polished, or too abstract.
- `0-59`: Weak match. Sounds unlike Nick's recent writing voice.

## Suggested evaluator feedback format

When critiquing a draft, the evaluator should return:

1. `score`
2. `verdict`
3. `what_matches`
4. `what_breaks_voice`
5. `revision_instructions`

The `revision_instructions` should be concrete, for example:

- "Replace the generic first paragraph with a specific moment from lived experience."
- "State the tension more plainly and earlier."
- "Cut the corporate phrasing and use simpler, more direct language."
- "End with a cleaner takeaway rather than a motivational flourish."

## Optional generator prompt notes

If this rubric is later used inside an evaluator-optimizer workflow, the
generator should be told to aim for these properties:

- write like someone thinking in public, not broadcasting a brand
- start concrete, then widen
- be willing to sound slightly sharp or amused
- prefer one memorable framing over several weak points
- preserve honesty over polish
- keep some velocity in the prose; do not over-edit away the pulse
